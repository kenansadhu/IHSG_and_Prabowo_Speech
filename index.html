Speech Rhetoric and Market Contraction: An Intraday Analysis of President Prabowo Subianto's Parliamentary Address on the Jakarta Stock Exchange (IHSG)

Research Paper Draft • Prepared for Journal Submission

Author: Kenan & Gemini Joint Research • Timeframe: May 20-21, 2026

Abstract

Background
Executive policy statements act as massive information vectors that adjust sovereign risk premiums and alter sector valuations in emerging capital markets.
Methodology
We synchronize 790 time-stamped subtitles from President Prabowo Subianto’s 2026 parliamentary address second-by-second with high-frequency 1-minute intraday tick data of the IHSG index. We test market movements against 5 specific thematic phases.
Key Findings
The IHSG experienced a significant intraday plunge of -2.66% ($6,387.12 to $6,217.52) in response to protectionist BUMN-only natural resource export regulations. This panic contraction was immediately offset by a spectacular +1.42% rebound when the administration pivoted to a pro-market "Indonesia Incorporated" partnership model, alongside a +0.41% rally on anti-corruption cleanup commitments.
Conclusion
Executive economic rhetoric is highly systemic. While protectionist regulatory threats trigger rapid asset sells, institutional partnerships and corruption purges are seen as market positives, bringing stability.

1. Introduction

Political economy literature increasingly recognizes that the rhetoric of major government executives acts as a potent transmission channel of sovereign policies into asset prices. In emerging markets like Indonesia, where state structures and regulations hold substantial influence over core sectors (commodities, energy, plantations), high-frequency capital asset markets exhibit highly elastic sensitivity to executive comments.

This paper examines the Rapat Paripurna address delivered by President Prabowo Subianto on May 20, 2026. The 99-minute speech was broadcasted live during Session 1 trading hours. Utilizing high-frequency data alignment, we outline how different thematic segments of the speech translated into immediate, statistically significant equity movements on the Jakarta Stock Exchange (IHSG).

2. Data and Empirical Methodology

The dataset features tick-by-tick 1-minute price entries of the IHSG. Subtitles from the speech’s official SRT file are synchronized via temporal alignment mapping $t_i \rightarrow t_{WIB}$. Standard statistical measures are applied to compute the cumulative phase returns:

R_Phase = (Price_End - Price_Start) / Price_Start * 100

To isolate distinct market triggers, the 99-minute address is partitioned into five distinct sub-phases: Phase A (Macroeconomy), Phase B (State Resource Control Shock), Phase C (Partnership Pivot), Phase D (Danantara Consolidation), and Phase E (Institutional Clean-up).

3. Empirical Analysis of Speech Phases

The empirical calculations present a stunning high-frequency mapping of President Prabowo Subianto's rhetorical narrative onto the IHSG index. In total, the index spanned a dramatic intraday peak-to-trough drop of -2.6553%, showing distinct inflection points.

3.1. Phase A: Macroeconomic Forecasts (10:25 - 11:05 WIB) • Net Return: -0.85%

During Phase A, the IHSG open price of 6,386.61 declined to 6,332.54 at 11:05 WIB. Although the 2027 RAPBN deficit target (1.80% - 2.40% of GDP) was moderate, the projection of a highly depreciated Rupiah range (up to Rp17,500/USD) signaled systemic inflationary pressures and external debt stress. This phase decomposes into two distinct market events:

  • 3.1.1. Sub-Phase A1: Fiscal Revenues & Sovereign Yields Baseline (10:25 - 10:45 WIB) • Return: -0.47%
    The introduction of 2027 revenue targets (11.82% - 12.40% of GDP) and SBN 10-year yield guidelines (6.5% - 7.3%) prompted immediate bondholder hedging, prompting a mild, orderly sell-off.
  • 3.1.2. Sub-Phase A2: Rupiah Target Depreciation Slide (10:45 - 11:05 WIB) • Return: -0.38%
    The announcement of a potential Rupiah depreciation ceiling of Rp17,500/USD sparked domestic inflation worries and imported cost concerns, escalating institutional sales.

3.2. Phase B: The BUMN Export Monopoly and Customs Shock (11:06 - 11:21 WIB) • Net Return: -1.56%

The primary macroeconomic panic of the session occurred during Phase B. The index plunged from 6,316.22 to its intraday trough of 6,217.52. This represented a capital loss of -1.56% in a 15-minute window, driven by resource nationalism and monopoly regulatory announcements:

  • 3.2.1. Sub-Phase B1: Customs Clean-up & Tax Attack (11:06 - 11:13 WIB) • Return: -1.07%
    A highly aggressive critique of Customs (Bea Cukai) corruption and the exposure of a $900 billion export trade under-invoicing leak raised fears of immediate regulatory audits and tax compliance cost spikes, precipitating a steep index-wide slide.
  • 3.2.2. Sub-Phase B2: SOE Export Monopoly PP Order (11:13 - 11:21 WIB) • Return: -0.50%
    Citing Article 33 of the Constitution, Prabowo announced a Government Regulation (PP) routing all palm oil, coal, and nickel exports strictly through government-designated BUMNs. Interpreting this as an effective trade nationalization, institutional investors aggressively liquidated resource equities, hitting the index bottom at 6,217.52.

3.3. Phase C: The "Indonesia Incorporated" Pivot (11:22 - 11:33 WIB) • Net Return: +1.42%

Following the bottom, the executive administration initiated an immediate rhetorical intervention to stabilize capital markets. The index surged +1.42% (6,219.92 to 6,308.07) in 11 minutes:

  • 3.3.1. Sub-Phase C1: Private Sector Growth Protection (11:22 - 11:27 WIB) • Return: +0.69%
    Immediate, explicit verbal guarantees that private enterprise must grow and remain protected ("swasta harus unggul") alleviated extreme expropriation fears, prompting immediate short-covering.
  • 3.3.2. Sub-Phase C2: Indonesia Incorporated Synergy (11:27 - 11:33 WIB) • Return: +0.72%
    The formal introduction of the "Indonesia Incorporated" state-private partnership model and credit programs for youth startups fostered a highly positive, collaborative sentiment, driving strong buying volume.

3.4. Phase D: Stabilization & Industrial Capacity Detail (11:34 - 11:52 WIB) • Net Return: +0.16%

As the speech turned toward long-term developmental frameworks, the index entered a stable consolidation phase, rising +0.16% (6,296.00 to 6,306.37):

  • 3.4.1. Sub-Phase D1: Danantara Sovereign Wealth Structuring (11:34 - 11:42 WIB) • Return: -0.14%
    Detail regarding the transfer of $1 trillion in national assets to the Danantara Superholding generated valuation debates among SOEs, leading to a minor cooling.
  • 3.4.2. Sub-Phase D2: Technology Localization & Supply Chain (11:42 - 11:52 WIB) • Return: +0.31%
    Commitments to secure domestic tech independence (manufacturing local cellphones, TVs, and PCs) attracted local industrial capital, bidding up technology and manufacturing shares.

3.5. Phase E: Deep State Purge & Final Closing (11:53 - 12:00 WIB) • Net Return: +0.41%

The address concluded with a powerful stance on anti-corruption, prompting a bullish +0.41% rally (6,306.44 to 6,332.18) at the close of Session 1:

  • 3.5.1. Sub-Phase E1: Bureaucratic Cleanup & Anti-Corruption (11:53 - 11:56 WIB) • Return: +0.47%
    Explicit pledges to target illegal networks protected by institutional "green or brown uniforms" were welcomed by investors as a structural commitment to the rule of law and property rights.
  • 3.5.2. Sub-Phase E2: Deep State Purge & Closing Call (11:56 - 11:59 WIB) • Return: -0.06%
    A final warning to the bureaucratic "deep state" prompted natural position closing at the Session 1 bell, securing the index close in highly supported territory.

4. Discussion & Financial Transmission Channels

The transmission channel of political rhetoric in Indonesian equities revolves around two main pillars:

  1. Resource Concentration: Commodities and resource companies represent a heavy concentration in the IHSG index. Direct interventionist threats regarding export structures trigger massive program sells.
  2. Regulatory Predictability vs. Sovereign Nationalism: International capital markets are highly elastic to risk. Hostile nationalist policies are heavily penalized. However, when framed as "Indonesia Incorporated"—an institutionalized partnership—risk premiums drop immediately.

5. Policy Recommendations & Conclusion

This study proves that political speeches act as double-edged swords on capital market indices. We advise economic communicators to present regulatory interventions alongside detailed private-sector partnership frameworks ("Indonesia Incorporated") in a synchronized manner, rather than separating them. Emphasizing clean, structured anti-corruption ("deep state" purge) campaigns serves as a strong market-positive that stabilizes long-term asset valuations.
